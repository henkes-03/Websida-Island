            Icelandic food

Iceland is surrounded by the sea, so it’s no surprise that fish and seafood are an important part of Icelandic cuisine. Most traditional Icelandic food revolves around fish, dairy products, bread, potatoes and lamb. The roots of Icelandic cuisine come from Scandinavian cuisine after the Vikings of Northern Europe settled there in the 9th century and thereafter. Today, restaurants in Reykjavik and other cities widely serve food from all over the world. Many restaurants specialize in seafood, focusing on product quality rather than traditional cooking methods. Classic icelandic foods: kjötsúpa, Harðfiskur, skyr, pylsur, brennivín, hákarl, Rúgbrauð.

            Family relations

Since Iceland is small, family is especially important. Icelandic households are generally larger than other Nordic countries.

Members of the extended family usually do not live together, for example cousins, but uncles, aunts and grandparents usually help with childcare. 

            Vikings

Iceland is a Nordic country, and it is believed that Nordic Vikings lived for the first time in the 9th century. The island’s connection with the Vikings can still be seen in some of its traditions.

The people of Iceland are proud of this heritage and have done a lot to protect it. A good example of this is modern Icelandic, which is still very similar to the old Nordic language used by its first inhabitants.
